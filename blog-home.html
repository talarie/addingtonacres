Before you get Bees

Beekeeping can be a rewarding and exciting experience. However for some it can be a task they take on and before they know it, it gets out of hand. Here’s some things to consider before entering the beekeeping business.

Do you have enough time? On top of the heaving learning you’ll have to do before and as you get the bees, you’ll have to check up on the hives every few weeks. If you’re running a homestead, this is another chore to add to the busy summer to-do list.

Are you allergic? Fairly easy question, does beekeeping put your life in danger? If so, consider hiring help to do this part of the workload.

Can you afford it? The equipment cost can pile up, and we do not recommend to skimp on quality here. Getting the right gear will be worth it in the long run once you’ll be making profit off the output, but the beginning it’s all about investment.

Caring for Cattle

If you’re new to owning cattle, there are some insider secrets you may be unaware of. First off, don’t get in over your head, many newcomers do. Cows are not low maintenance, if anyone told you they are, they were lying.

Establish your first paddocks early on, and don’t be picky. You’ll probably change your design before finishing up a permanent version. Try getting an aerial photo of your land so you can plot out exactly what you want. Make sure to make it long and narrow, cattle do not like square pastures, let me tell you.

Finally, don’t forget to keep the pasture mowed to avoid overgrowth and weeds. This is very important for the health of your pasture.

Organic Fertilizers for your Garden

Having quality fertilizer doesn’t have to cost you an arm and a leg. You can make your own organic fertilizer that will work even better than store bought stuff! Here are some of our suggestions…

The easiest organic fertilizer is compost. This can include lawn clippings, kitchen scraps, and more. Crushed eggshells and coffee grinds have proved to be outstanding ingredients.

Another idea is wood ashes, although they must be from untreated wood.

Manure is another popular choice. An important lesson to learn is to only use dried out, rotted manure, as unrotted manure can cause burning of the roots of your plants.

Finally, consider planting a few nitrogen-producing plants, such as crimson clover or alfalfa.

Collecting and Cleaning Fresh Eggs

We believe you should only sell quality products you are proud of. That’s why we can’t stress that you’re eggs are fresh, clean and safe. Here are some tips to ensure you get the best product and keep your animals happy.

Try collecting as much as possible, twice a day works for us! This keeps them from being eaten or broken by the chickens. In general, keep the environment clean and dry as much as you can.

You can dry clean (wiping the eggs with a hard sponge or sandpaper to remove dirt and feces) or wet clean (run under tap water, dried with paper towel and sanitized with diluted bleach) your eggs.

Finally, store your eggs in a clean and dry environment. Package them in cartons and label the collection date, then put them in a fridge. Don’t forget they are good for one month!
